Exercise 3. Types of Formulas (*)

Use truth tables (and, if needed, the equivalences seen during the lecture) to verify whether the following formulas are tautologies, contradictions, or non-tautological satisfiable formulas.

  1. \( \neg x \to x \to \neg x \)
  2. \( (x \to y) \land (\neg y \to \neg x) \)
  3. \( (x \to \neg y) \to (\neg y \to \neg x) \)
  4. \( x \land y \lor (\neg x \land \neg y) \lor z \)

Solution

Goal: Determine if the formulas are Tautologies (always true), Contradictions (always false), or Satisfiable (sometimes true, sometimes false).

1. \( \neg x \to (x \to \neg x) \)

  • Simplification: Recall that \( A \to B \equiv \neg A \lor B \).
  • \( x \to \neg x \equiv \neg x \lor \neg x \equiv \neg x \)
  • \( \neg x \to \neg x \equiv \neg(\neg x) \lor \neg x \equiv x \lor \neg x \equiv \top \)
  • Result: Tautology

2. \( (x \to y) \land (\neg y \to \neg x) \)

  • Simplification: \( (\neg y \to \neg x) \) is the contrapositive of \( (x \to y) \), so they mean the same thing logically.
  • The formula is just \( (x \to y) \land (x \to y) \equiv x \to y \).
  • Result: Satisfiable (True when \( x=0 \), false when \( x=1, y=0 \)).

3. \( (x \to \neg y) \to (\neg y \to \neg x) \)

  • Test values: Let \( x = 1, y = 1 \).
    • \( (1 \to 0) \to (0 \to 0) \implies 0 \to 1 \implies 1 \)
  • Test values: Let \( x = 1, y = 0 \).
    • \( (1 \to 1) \to (1 \to 0) \implies 1 \to 0 \implies 0 \)
  • Result: Satisfiable (Not a tautology because it can evaluate to false).

4. \( x \land y \lor (\neg x \land \neg y) \lor z \)

  • Interpretation: True if \( x \) and \( y \) share the same value (both true or both false) OR if \( z \) is true. False if \( x \neq y \) and \( z = 0 \).
  • Result: Satisfiable

Exercise 4. Expressivity (**)

Can every propositional knowledge base be expressed as a propositional formula?

If the answer is yes, argue why; if it is no, give at least two examples of KBs which cannot be expressed.

Solution

Answer: YES.

Reasoning: A propositional Knowledge Base is fundamentally a set of propositional clauses (like a set of Horn clauses). Because the KB assumes all its facts and rules must hold true simultaneously, the entire KB can be represented mathematically as the conjunction (\( \land \)) of all its rules. Since combining formulas with logical ANDs just creates one large well-formed propositional formula, any propositional KB is expressible as a single propositional formula.

Exercise 6. Reasoning (*)

Consider the following knowledge base \( K \):

\( u \gets \)
\( x \gets v \)
\( z \gets v, x \)
\( z \gets w, x \)
\( p \gets \)
\( r \gets s, t \)
\( y \gets x, z, u \)
\( u \gets y, z \)
\( z \gets y, u \)
\( v \gets \)
\( t \gets w, u \)
\( \gets z, s \)
  1. Compute the redux \( \widehat{K} \)
  2. Find all the facts that are entailed by \( K \)
  3. Decide whether the following clauses are consequences of \( K \):
    • a) \( t \gets x \)
    • b) \( q \gets q \)
    • c) \( r \gets y, s \)
    • d) \( \gets x \)

Solution

Goal: Process the KB using forward chaining and structural simplification.

1. Compute the redux \( \widehat{K} \):

The "redux" is the KB after we remove all rules that contain variables in their body which can never be proven. Looking at the KB, the atoms \( w \) and \( s \) do not appear as the "head" of any valid rule, so they are unprovable (always false). We cross out any rule relying on \( w \) or \( s \):

  • Cross out: \( z \gets w, x \) ; \( r \gets s, t \) ; \( t \gets w, u \) ; \( \gets z, s \)
  • \( \widehat{K} \) becomes: \( u \gets \), \( x \gets v \), \( z \gets v, x \), \( p \gets \), \( y \gets x, z, u \), \( u \gets y, z \), \( z \gets y, u \), \( v \gets \).

2. Entailed Facts (Forward Chaining):

  • Base facts: \( u, v, p \)
  • Using \( v \), rule \( x \gets v \) gives \( x \).
  • Using \( v, x \), rule \( z \gets v, x \) gives \( z \).
  • Using \( x, z, u \), rule \( y \gets x, z, u \) gives \( y \).
  • Entailed: \( u, v, p, x, z, y \)

3. Consequences:

  • a) \( t \gets x \) (NO): \( x \) is true, but \( t \) is not entailed by the KB. So \( x \implies t \) is false.
  • b) \( q \gets q \) (YES): \( q \implies q \) is a tautology (always true).
  • c) \( r \gets y, s \) (YES): This means \( y \land s \implies r \). Since \( s \) is unprovable (false in all models), the premise \( y \land s \) is always false. A false premise makes an implication vacuously true.
  • d) \( \gets x \) (NO): This means \( \neg x \). Since \( x \) is entailed (always true), \( \neg x \) is always false.

Exercise 7. Formulas (***)

During the lecture we saw a method to transform any Boolean function into a formula (see Question 5). This method is very inefficient if the formula maps many valuations to true.

Devise a method that works better in this scenario.
Hint: recall the properties of propositional formulas.

Solution

Goal: Devise an efficient method to get a formula from a Boolean function when it maps many valuations to True.

Method: Standard DNF (sum of minterms) is inefficient if there are many True rows. Instead:

  1. Target the False rows to build a DNF formula for the negation of the function (\( \neg \varphi \)).
  2. Then, negate that entire formula to get \( \varphi \).
  3. Use De Morgan's Laws to push the negations inward, converting it into a much shorter Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF).

From the Professor's Example:

Assume the function is False at 000 and 111.

  • \( \neg \varphi = (\neg x \land \neg y \land \neg z) \lor (x \land y \land z) \)
  • \( \varphi = \neg( (\neg x \land \neg y \land \neg z) \lor (x \land y \land z) ) \)
  • Apply De Morgan's: \( \varphi = (x \lor y \lor z) \land (\neg x \lor \neg y \lor \neg z) \)

Exercise 8. Tautologies (***)

The soundness of the fact extraction algorithm seen during the lecture (unit propagation) depends on the following property:

if \( \varphi \to \psi \) and \( \psi \to \xi \) are both tautologies, then \( \varphi \to \xi \) is also a tautology.

Show that this property holds always in propositional logic.

Solution

Goal: Prove transitivity of implication: If \( \varphi \to \psi \) and \( \psi \to \xi \) are tautologies, then \( \varphi \to \xi \) is a tautology.

Semantic Proof:

We use a valuation function \( \nu() \) where \( 1 \) is true and \( 0 \) is false.

  1. Assume \( \nu(\varphi \to \psi) = 1 \) and \( \nu(\psi \to \xi) = 1 \) for all assignments.
  2. We want to prove \( \nu(\varphi \to \xi) = 1 \).
  3. If \( \nu(\varphi) = 0 \), then \( \nu(\varphi \to \xi) = 1 \) trivially (false implies anything).
  4. If \( \nu(\varphi) = 1 \):
    • Because \( \nu(\varphi \to \psi) = 1 \), it must be that \( \nu(\psi) = 1 \).
    • Because \( \nu(\psi \to \xi) = 1 \) and we know \( \nu(\psi) = 1 \), it must be that \( \nu(\xi) = 1 \).
  5. Since \( \nu(\varphi)=1 \) logically necessitates \( \nu(\xi)=1 \), the implication \( \nu(\varphi \to \xi) = 1 \) always holds.

Thus, it is a tautology.

Exercise 9. KE (**)

  1. Construct a KB expressing the following knowledge (decide first which propositional variables to use):
    • platypi are monotremata
    • monotremata are mammals and oviparous
    • hairy animals are not oviparous
    • hairy animals which are mammals are venomous
  2. Are hairy (animal) monotremata also platypi? Justify
  3. Are hairy (animal) monotremata also whales? Justify

Solution

Goal: Construct a KB from English sentences and reason with it. Let's use variables: \( p \) (platypus), \( m \) (monotreme), \( a \) (mammal), \( o \) (oviparous), \( h \) (hairy), \( v \) (venomous).

1. Construct KB:

  • Platypi are monotremata: \( m \gets p \)
  • Monotremata are mammals and oviparous: \( a \gets m \) and \( o \gets m \)
  • Hairy animals are not oviparous: \( \gets h, o \) (meaning \( h \land o \implies False \))
  • Hairy animals which are mammals are venomous: \( v \gets h, a \)

2. Are hairy (animal) monotremata also platypi? (YES)

  • Justification: Assume we have an animal that is both hairy (\( h \)) and a monotreme (\( m \)). From \( m \), our KB infers it is oviparous (\( o \gets m \)). Now we have a creature that is both hairy (\( h \)) and oviparous (\( o \)). However, our KB specifically forbids this (\( \gets h, o \)). This creates a logical contradiction (False).
  • By the Principle of Explosion (ex falso quodlibet), anything follows from a contradiction. Therefore, a hairy monotreme mathematically entails being a platypus.

3. Are hairy (animal) monotremata also whales? (YES)

  • Justification: YES, for the exact same reason. The premise "hairy monotreme" breaks the logical rules of our KB, generating a contradiction. From a contradiction, any conclusion—even being a whale—is logically entailed.

Exercise 1. Knowledge Representation (*)

Assume that all facts in a knowledge base will have the forms:

child(a, b) <-
female(a) <-

meaning that “a is a child of b,” and “a is female,” respectively.

  1. Using predicate rules, create a knowledge base which describes family relations including at least: niece, grandparent, sibling, cousin, and ancestor.

  2. If, in addition, facts of the form married(a, b) <- are allowed, extend the knowledge base to allow legal family within the notions of grandmother, uncle, and co-father-in-law.

Solution

1. Family relations using predicate rules

Variables in rules are denoted with uppercase letters. The fact child(X, Y) means "X is a child of Y", implying Y is the parent.

parent(X, Y) <- child(Y, X)
sibling(X, Y) <- child(X, Z), child(Y, Z)
niece(X, Y) <- female(X), child(X, Z), sibling(Z, Y)
grandparent(X, Y) <- child(Y, Z), child(Z, X)
cousin(X, Y) <- child(X, Z), child(Y, W), sibling(Z, W)
ancestor(X, Y) <- child(Y, X)
ancestor(X, Y) <- child(Y, Z), ancestor(X, Z)

2. Legal family additions

We establish a symmetric spouse relation and define a legal_parent as either a biological parent or a step-parent (the spouse of a biological parent).

spouse(X, Y) <- married(X, Y)
spouse(X, Y) <- married(Y, X)

legal_parent(X, Y) <- child(Y, X)
legal_parent(X, Y) <- child(Y, Z), spouse(X, Z)
legal_sibling(X, Y) <- legal_parent(Z, X), legal_parent(Z, Y)

grandmother(X, Y) <- female(X), legal_parent(X, Z), legal_parent(Z, Y)

uncle(X, Y) <- legal_sibling(X, Z), legal_parent(Z, Y)
uncle(X, Y) <- spouse(X, W), legal_sibling(W, Z), legal_parent(Z, Y)

co_father_in_law(X, Y) <- legal_parent(X, Z), spouse(Z, W), legal_parent(Y, W)

(Note: Since standard Datalog lacks a built-in male predicate and negation, gender is left implicit for uncle and co_father_in_law according to standard Logic Programming limitations).

Exercise 2. Consistency (**)

We call a (predicate) knowledge base positive if it has no constraints. Tell whether the following statements are true or false, justifying your answer.

  1. Every KB is consistent
  2. Every positive KB is consistent
  3. Every positive KB has a model with one element; that is, with domain \(\Delta = \{\delta\}\)
  4. Every consistent KB has a model with one element

Solution

  1. False. A KB can contain constraints (like <- P(x), Q(x)) that might be violated by the base facts, making the KB inconsistent.

  2. True. A positive KB (one with no constraints or negations) always has at least one model—for instance, the maximal Herbrand model where all predicates are true for all constants.

  3. True. Without constraints (like Unique Name Assumption or negative clauses) preventing it, you can safely map all constants to a single domain element \(\Delta = \{\delta\}\) and assign all relations to be true. No contradictions can occur.

  4. False. A consistent KB may contain structural constraints (e.g., <- P(x, y), P(y, x) alongside facts P(a,b) and P(b,c)) forcing elements to be distinct. Mapping them to a single element would violate the constraints.

Exercise 5. Constraints (**)

Add the fact child(hans, ingrid) to the KB of Exercise 3, and answer the following queries:

  1. ancestor(efraim, efraim)
  2. ancestor(fiona, claudio)

Analyse these results in the context of KR and, if needed, add constraints to remove unexpected consequences.

Solution

Adding child(hans, ingrid) means ingrid is the parent of hans, introducing a cycle: ingrid → denis → hans → ingrid.

  1. ancestor(efraim, efraim): False (efraim has no children, so he is not an ancestor of anyone).
  2. ancestor(fiona, claudio): False.

KR Analysis & Constraint:

While the specific queries returned False, the addition logically breaks the Knowledge Base because individuals involved in the cycle (like ingrid) become ancestors of themselves (e.g., ancestor(ingrid, ingrid) evaluates to True). This violates the biological/hierarchical nature of ancestry and causes infinite loops in top-down query engines (like Prolog).

To remove this unexpected consequence, we add the irreflexivity constraint:

<- ancestor(X, X)

This will automatically render any model containing cyclic family sub-graphs logically inconsistent.

Exercise 6. Query Expressivity (***)

Suppose that we are interested in deducing whether a rule <- Q(x) is entailed by a KB \(K\); that is, whether every model of \(K\) also satisfies this rule.

Devise a reasoning method that can derive this consequence using the tools that we have seen in the lecture, or explain why it cannot be done.

Solution

Yes, it can be done. A rule with an empty head <- Q(x) represents the constraint \(\forall x \neg Q(x)\). In logic programming, entailed constraints can be checked via Refutation / Consistency Checking.

Reasoning Method:

  • Introduce a new, fresh Skolem constant (e.g., sk) that does not appear anywhere in \(K\).
  • Add the fact Q(sk) <- to \(K\).
  • Evaluate whether the resulting KB (\(K \cup \{Q(sk)\}\)) is inconsistent.
  • If the new KB is inconsistent, it means adding \(Q\) for any element creates a contradiction. Therefore, \(K\) logically entails the rule <- Q(x). If it remains consistent, it does not entail it.

Exercise 7. Disjoint unions (**)

Consider two interpretations \(\mathcal{I} = (\Delta^\mathcal{I}, \cdot^\mathcal{I})\) and \(\mathcal{J} = (\Delta^\mathcal{J}, \cdot^\mathcal{J})\) such that \(\Delta^\mathcal{I} \cap \Delta^\mathcal{J} = \emptyset\).

The disjoint union of \(\mathcal{I}\) and \(\mathcal{J}\) is the interpretation \(\mathcal{I} \oplus \mathcal{J} = (\Delta^\mathcal{I} \cup \Delta^\mathcal{J}, \cdot^{\mathcal{I}\mathcal{J}})\) where for every predicate \(P\), \(P^{\mathcal{I}\mathcal{J}} = P^\mathcal{I} \cup P^\mathcal{J}\).

In other words, the disjoint union of \(\mathcal{I}\) and \(\mathcal{J}\) is the graph obtained by putting together the two graphs defined by \(\mathcal{I}\) and \(\mathcal{J}\).

Is it true that if \(\mathcal{I}\) and \(\mathcal{J}\) are both models of a predicate knowledge base \(K\), then \(\mathcal{I} \oplus \mathcal{J}\) is also a model of \(K\)? Justify.

Solution

False. The disjoint union \(\mathcal{I} \oplus \mathcal{J}\) is not necessarily a model.

Justification (Counterexample):

Consider a KB with a single rule containing unconnected body variables:

H(x) <- P(x), Q(y)
  • Let interpretation \(\mathcal{I}\) have domain \(\{1\}\) where \(Q(1)\) is True, but \(P(1)\) and \(H(1)\) are False. \(\mathcal{I}\) is a model because the rule's body is false.
  • Let interpretation \(\mathcal{J}\) have domain \(\{2\}\) where \(P(2)\) is True, but \(Q(2)\) and \(H(2)\) are False. \(\mathcal{J}\) is a model because the rule's body is false.
  • In \(\mathcal{I} \oplus \mathcal{J}\), the domain is \(\{1, 2\}\). Here, \(Q(1)\) is True, and \(P(2)\) is True.

If we assign \(x=2\) and \(y=1\), the body P(2), Q(1) becomes completely True. However, \(H(2)\) remains False. Thus, the rule is violated, making \(\mathcal{I} \oplus \mathcal{J}\) not a valid model.

Exercise 8. Model sizes with constraints (***)

  1. Build a knowledge base \(K\) such that all models have at least 3 elements or explain why it is not possible.
  2. Build a knowledge base \(K\) such that all models have at most 5 elements or explain why it is not possible.

Solution

1. KB where all models have at least 3 elements: Possible

We use an irreflexive, asymmetric relation to force a minimal cycle size:

E(a, b) <-
E(b, c) <-
<- E(X, X)
<- E(X, Y), E(Y, X)

If a model had only 1 or 2 elements, by the Pigeonhole Principle, at least two of the constants {a, b, c} must map to the same domain element. If \(a=b\), it violates <- E(X, X). If \(a=c\), E(a,b) and E(b,c) effectively become a 2-cycle E(a,b) and E(b,a), which violates <- E(X, Y), E(Y, X). Therefore, any valid model must maintain at least 3 strictly distinct elements.

2. KB where all models have at most 5 elements: Not possible

Reason: In relational First-Order Logic languages without an explicit, extensional equality/identity predicate (=), there is no capability to bound model sizes from above. For any consistent model \(M\), one can simply construct a larger model \(M'\) by duplicating an existing element \(d\) into \(d'\), copying over all exact relationships. Since there's no way to establish an "identity constraint" preventing duplicates, \(M'\) will remain a logically perfectly satisfying model. Thus, any consistent KB can scale infinitely.

Exercise 1. Homomorphism (**)

Consider the predicate KB containing the rules

\(Q(x, y) \leftarrow Q(x, z), Q(z, y)\) \(\leftarrow Q(x, x)\)
\(Q(a, b) \leftarrow\) \(Q(b, c) \leftarrow\)

  1. Construct, if possible, (i) the canonical interpretation \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\), (ii) a model \(\mathcal{I}_1\) with four objects, and (iii) a model \(\mathcal{I}_2\) with two objects. If not possible, explain why.
  2. Build the homomorphisms from \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\) to \(\mathcal{I}_1\) and \(\mathcal{I}_2\)
  3. Find at least one fact that holds in \(\mathcal{I}_1\) but not in \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\), or explain why it is not possible
  4. Find at least one fact that holds in \(\mathcal{I}_2\) but not in \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\), or explain why it is not possible

Solution

KB Analysis:

  • Rules: \(Q(x, y) \leftarrow Q(x, z), Q(z, y)\) (Transitivity) and \(\leftarrow Q(x, x)\) (Irreflexivity).
  • Facts: \(Q(a, b)\) and \(Q(b, c)\).

1. Constructing Interpretations

  • (i) Canonical Interpretation \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\):
    Starting from the facts \(Q(a,b)\) and \(Q(b,c)\), applying transitivity yields \(Q(a,c)\). No self-loops are generated, so irreflexivity is satisfied.
    \(\Delta^{\mathcal{I}_{can}} = \{a, b, c\}\)
    \(Q^{\mathcal{I}_{can}} = \{(a, b), (b, c), (a, c)\}\)
  • (ii) A model \(\mathcal{I}_1\) with 4 objects:
    We can add a new disconnected element \(d\) to \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\), or add an outgoing edge from \(c\) to \(d\). Let's define it as:
    \(\Delta^{\mathcal{I}_1} = \{a, b, c, d\}\)
    \(Q^{\mathcal{I}_1} = \{(a, b), (b, c), (a, c), (c, d), (b, d), (a, d)\}\) (This preserves both transitivity and irreflexivity).
  • (iii) A model \(\mathcal{I}_2\) with 2 objects:
    Not possible. By the Pigeonhole Principle, mapping 3 constants \(\{a, b, c\}\) to a domain of 2 objects means at least two constants must map to the same object.
    • If \(a=b\), \(Q(a,b) \implies Q(a,a)\) (violates \(\leftarrow Q(x,x)\)).
    • If \(b=c\), \(Q(b,c) \implies Q(b,b)\) (violates \(\leftarrow Q(x,x)\)).
    • If \(a=c\), \(Q(a,c) \implies Q(a,a)\) (violates \(\leftarrow Q(x,x)\)).

2. Homomorphisms

  • \(h_1: \mathcal{I}_{can} \to \mathcal{I}_1\) is the identity mapping: \(h_1(a)=a\), \(h_1(b)=b\), \(h_1(c)=c\).
  • \(h_2: \mathcal{I}_{can} \to \mathcal{I}_2\) is not possible to build, since the interpretation \(\mathcal{I}_2\) cannot exist.

3. Fact in \(\mathcal{I}_1\) but not in \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\)

Using the \(\mathcal{I}_1\) defined above, the fact \(Q(c, d)\) holds in \(\mathcal{I}_1\) but not in \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\).
(Note: If strictly limited to the original signature \(\{a, b, c\}\), it is logically impossible to add any new positive ground fact. Adding any edge amongst \(a, b, c\) creates a cycle, which by transitivity would infer a self-loop, violating the irreflexivity constraint).

4. Fact in \(\mathcal{I}_2\) but not in \(\mathcal{I}_{can}\)

Not possible. Since the model \(\mathcal{I}_2\) itself cannot exist, we cannot evaluate any facts on it.

Exercise 2. Consistency (*)

Tell which of the following \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) concepts are satisfiable and which are unsatisfiable:

  1. \(\exists r.A \sqcap \exists r.C\)
  2. \(\exists s.(A \sqcap \exists s.B)\)
  3. \(A \sqcap \exists s.(\bot \sqcap \exists r.\top) \sqcap \exists r.B\)
  4. \(C \sqcap D \sqcap \top \sqcap \exists r.(\top \sqcap B)\)
  5. \(\exists s.\exists s.(\bot \sqcap B)\)

Solution

Recall that \(\exists r.\bot \equiv \bot\) and \(X \sqcap \bot \equiv \bot\).

  1. \(\exists r.A \sqcap \exists r.C\) — Satisfiable. (A node can have two distinct \(r\)-successors, one in \(A\) and one in \(C\)).
  2. \(\exists s.(A \sqcap \exists s.B)\) — Satisfiable.
  3. \(A \sqcap \exists s.(\bot \sqcap \exists r.\top) \sqcap \exists r.B\) — Unsatisfiable. (Contains \(\bot\) inside the \(\exists s\) restriction, collapsing the branch to \(\exists s.\bot \equiv \bot\)).
  4. \(C \sqcap D \sqcap \top \sqcap \exists r.(\top \sqcap B)\) — Satisfiable.
  5. \(\exists s.\exists s.(\bot \sqcap B)\) — Unsatisfiable. (Contains \(\bot\), propagating upwards to make the whole concept \(\bot\)).

Verify whether the following subsumption relations hold:

  2. \(\exists r.(A \sqcap \exists s.\top) \sqsubseteq \exists s.\exists r.B\)
  3. \(\exists r.(B \sqcap \exists s.\top) \sqsubseteq \exists r.\exists s.\top\)
  4. \(\exists r.(A \sqcap \exists r.\bot) \sqsubseteq \exists s.B\)
  5. \(A \sqcap \exists r.B \sqsubseteq \exists r.(\bot \sqcap A)\)

  1. \(A \sqcap C \sqcap \exists s.B \sqsubseteq C \sqcap \exists s.\top\) — True (\(\exists s.B\) is trivially subsumed by \(\exists s.\top\)).

  6. \(\exists s.(B \sqcap \exists s.\bot) \sqsubseteq B \sqcap \exists r.\bot \sqcap \exists t.(A \sqcap B)\) — True (LHS collapses to \(\bot\)).

  8. \(B \sqcap \exists r.(A \sqcap C) \sqcap \exists r.(B \sqcap C) \sqsubseteq B \sqcap \exists r.A \sqcap \exists r.B \sqcap \exists r.C\) — True (\(\exists r.(A \sqcap C)\) implies both \(\exists r.A\) and \(\exists r.C\)).

\(\mathcal{T} = \{ \top \sqsubseteq \exists r.A,\;\; \exists r.A \sqsubseteq \exists r.B,\;\; A \sqcap C \sqsubseteq \bot,\;\; A \sqcap B \sqsubseteq \bot,\;\; \exists r.B \sqsubseteq \exists s.C \}\)

  2. Construct a model of \(\mathcal{T}\) which has at least five elements
  3. Construct a model of \(\mathcal{T}\) which has at most three elements

\(\Delta = \{1, 2, 3\}\). Let \(A=\{1\}\), \(B=\{2\}\), \(C=\{3\}\).
\(r = \{(1,1), (2,1), (3,1), (1,2), (2,2), (3,2)\}\)
\(s = \{(1,3), (2,3), (3,3)\}\)

\(\Delta = \{1, 2\}\). Let \(A=\{1\}\), \(B=\{2\}\), \(C=\{2\}\).
\(r = \{(1,1), (1,2), (2,1), (2,2)\}\)
\(s = \{(1,2), (2,2)\}\)

Exercise 6. Disjoint Unions (***)

Recall the notion of disjoint union of interpretations from the previous exercise session. Is the disjoint union of two models of an \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) TBox \(\mathcal{T}\) also a model of \(\mathcal{T}\)? Justify

Solution

Yes, it is a model.

Justification: The semantics of all \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) constructs (conjunctions \(\sqcap\) and existential restrictions \(\exists\)) strictly preserve their extensions under disjoint unions.

For two interpretations \(\mathcal{I}_1, \mathcal{I}_2 \models \mathcal{T}\), construct \(\mathcal{I} = \mathcal{I}_1 \uplus \mathcal{I}_2\).
By structural induction, for any concept \(C\), its extension evaluates cleanly isolated due to non-intersecting domains: \(C^{\mathcal{I}} = C^{\mathcal{I}_1} \uplus C^{\mathcal{I}_2}\).

For any TBox axiom \(C \sqsubseteq D\), since \(\mathcal{I}_1 \models C \sqsubseteq D \implies C^{\mathcal{I}_1} \subseteq D^{\mathcal{I}_1}\), and identically for \(\mathcal{I}_2\), the set-theoretic union preserves the subset relation: \(C^{\mathcal{I}_1} \uplus C^{\mathcal{I}_2} \subseteq D^{\mathcal{I}_1} \uplus D^{\mathcal{I}_2} \implies C^{\mathcal{I}} \subseteq D^{\mathcal{I}}\).
Thus, \(\mathcal{I} \models \mathcal{T}\).

Exercise 7. Model Size (***)

Construct an \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) TBox that has only models with at most 4 elements, or argue why it cannot exist.

Solution

Argument why it cannot exist:

A satisfiable \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) TBox limiting its models to at most 4 elements cannot exist.

By the exact principle proven in Exercise 6, the \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) logic fragment satisfies the disjoint union property. If a satisfiable TBox \(\mathcal{T}\) has a valid model \(\mathcal{I}\) (which by DL standard definitions must have a non-empty domain, so \(|\Delta^\mathcal{I}| \ge 1\)), we can repeatedly take disjoint unions of the model with itself to produce new models.

For instance, the disjoint union of 5 copies of \(\mathcal{I}\) (i.e. \(\mathcal{I} \uplus \mathcal{I} \uplus \mathcal{I} \uplus \mathcal{I} \uplus \mathcal{I}\)) guarantees a model mathematically identical logically but containing strictly \(\ge 5\) elements. Therefore, the logic inherently lacks the expressive power (like maximum cardinality constraints or nominals) to impose finite upper bounds on model sizes.

(Note: An unsatisfiable TBox like \(\mathcal{T} = \{\top \sqsubseteq \bot\}\) trivially has 0 models, which is technically "at most 4", but it's fundamentally incapable of producing valid bounded structures).

Exercise 1. Normalisation

Transform the following \(\mathcal{EL}^\perp\) TBox \(\mathcal{T}\) into normal form and apply the completion algorithm until saturation.

\(\mathcal{T} = \{\)
    \(C \sqsubseteq \exists r.A,\)
    \(\exists r.\top \sqsubseteq B \sqcap \exists r.B,\)
    \(B \sqcap C \sqsubseteq \bot,\)
    \(\exists r.B \sqsubseteq \exists s.C,\)
    \(B \sqsubseteq \exists s.(B \sqcap D),\)
    \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.B\)
\(\}\)

Solution Breakdown

1. Normalisation Step

In \(\mathcal{EL}\), normal form requires axioms to be shaped like: \(A_1 \sqcap A_2 \sqsubseteq B\), \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.B\), or \(\exists r.A \sqsubseteq B\) (where \(A, B\) are concept names or \(\top\)). We introduce new variables (\(X_1, X_2, X_3\)) to split complex axioms.

  • \(C \sqsubseteq \exists r.A \rightarrow\) (Already in normal form)
  • \(\exists r.\top \sqsubseteq B \sqcap \exists r.B \rightarrow\) Splits into:
    • \(\exists r.\top \sqsubseteq B\)
    • \(\exists r.\top \sqsubseteq X_1\)
    • \(X_1 \sqsubseteq \exists r.B\)
  • \(B \sqcap C \sqsubseteq \bot \rightarrow\) (Already in normal form)
  • \(\exists r.B \sqsubseteq \exists s.C \rightarrow\) Splits into:
    • \(\exists r.B \sqsubseteq X_2\)
    • \(X_2 \sqsubseteq \exists s.C\)
  • \(B \sqsubseteq \exists s.(B \sqcap D) \rightarrow\) Splits into:
    • \(B \sqsubseteq \exists s.X_3\)
    • \(X_3 \sqsubseteq B\)
    • \(X_3 \sqsubseteq D\)
  • \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.B \rightarrow\) (Already in normal form)

2. Completion Algorithm (Saturation)

Applying the saturation rules reveals logical consequences (unsatisfiabilities). Following the chain of inferences:

  1. From \(C \sqsubseteq \exists r.A\), any instance of \(C\) has an \(r\)-successor.
  2. Because \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.\top\) is trivially true for instances of \(A\), and the TBox says \(\exists r.\top \sqsubseteq B\), it follows that \(A \sqsubseteq B\).
  3. Similarly, from \(C \sqsubseteq \exists r.A\), we get \(C \sqsubseteq \exists r.\top\), which implies \(C \sqsubseteq B\).
  4. Since \(C \sqsubseteq B\), the intersection \(B \sqcap C\) is simply \(C\). The TBox states \(B \sqcap C \sqsubseteq \bot\), which means \(C \sqsubseteq \bot\). (\(C\) is unsatisfiable).
  5. If \(C\) is unsatisfiable, then \(\exists s.C\) is unsatisfiable (\(\bot\)).
  6. Tracing backward: \(X_2 \sqsubseteq \exists s.C \implies X_2 \sqsubseteq \bot\).
  7. \(\exists r.B \sqsubseteq X_2 \implies \exists r.B \sqsubseteq \bot\).
  8. \(X_1 \sqsubseteq \exists r.B \implies X_1 \sqsubseteq \bot\).
  9. \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.B \implies A \sqsubseteq \bot\). (\(A\) is unsatisfiable).

Exercise 4. ALC Satisfiability

Decide whether the following \(\mathcal{ALC}\) concepts are satisfiable or not. If they are, provide a satisfying interpretation.

  1. \(\neg B \sqcup \exists s.\bot\)
  2. \(D \sqcap \neg \forall s.(B \sqcup C) \sqcap \neg \exists s.\neg C\)
  3. \(B \sqcap (\neg B \sqcap \exists s.\bot) \sqcup \forall r.\bot\)
  4. \(\forall r.(A \sqcup B) \sqcap \neg(\forall r.A) \sqcap \exists s.\neg B\)
  5. \(\exists r.\exists s.\forall s.\bot\)
  6. \(\exists r.\forall s.\exists r.\top\)
  7. \(\forall r.\exists r.\exists s.\bot\)

Solution Breakdown

Satisfiability checks whether a concept can have at least one instance without creating logical contradictions.

  1. \(\neg B \sqcup \exists s.\bot\): Satisfiable. Equivalent to \(\neg B\). Model: A single node not in \(B\).
  2. \(D \sqcap \neg \forall s.(B \sqcup C) \sqcap \neg \exists s.\neg C\): Unsatisfiable. Equivalent to \(D \sqcap \exists s.(\neg B \sqcap \neg C) \sqcap \forall s.C\). It requires an \(s\)-successor to be \(\neg C\), but also requires all \(s\)-successors to be \(C\). Contradiction.
  3. \(B \sqcap (\neg B \sqcap \exists s.\bot) \sqcup \forall r.\bot\): Satisfiable. The first part is \(\bot\) (since \(B \sqcap \neg B\)), leaving \(\forall r.\bot\). Model: A single node with no \(r\)-successors.
  4. \(\forall r.(A \sqcup B) \sqcap \neg(\forall r.A) \sqcap \exists s.\neg B\): Satisfiable. Model: Node 1 has an \(r\)-successor (Node 2) that is \(B, \neg A\), and an \(s\)-successor (Node 3) that is \(A, \neg B\).
  5. \(\exists r.\exists s.\forall s.\bot\): Satisfiable. Model: Node 1 \(\xrightarrow{r}\) Node 2 \(\xrightarrow{s}\) Node 3. Node 3 has no outgoing \(s\)-edges.
  6. \(\exists r.\forall s.\exists r.\top\): Satisfiable. Model: Node 1 \(\xrightarrow{r}\) Node 2 \(\xrightarrow{s}\) Node 3 \(\xrightarrow{r}\) Node 4.
  7. \(\forall r.\exists r.\exists s.\bot\): Satisfiable. Because \(\exists s.\bot \equiv \bot\), this reduces to \(\forall r.\bot\). Model: A single node with no outgoing \(r\)-edges.

Exercise 7. TBox Consistency

  2. \(\mathcal{T}_2 = \{A \sqsubseteq \exists s.B,\ \forall s.\neg B \sqsubseteq A,\ \exists r.B \sqsubseteq \forall r.\neg B\}\)

Solution Breakdown

A TBox is consistent if there exists at least one valid model (even an empty one, as long as no global contradictions are enforced on all nodes).

  2. \(\mathcal{T}_2\): Consistent. Model: Node 1 (\(A\)) points via \(s\) to Node 2 (\(B\)). Node 2 loops to itself via \(s\) to avoid triggering the \(\forall s.\neg B \sqsubseteq A\) rule.
  3. \(\mathcal{T}_3\): Consistent. Model: Node 2 (\(A\)) points via \(r\) to Node 1 (\(\neg A\)). Node 1 points via \(s\) to Node 2 (\(A\)).
  4. \(\mathcal{T}_4\): Inconsistent. \(\forall r.\top \sqsubseteq A\) forces nodes with no \(r\)-successors to be in \(A\). But if they are in \(A\), they must have an \(r\)-successor (due to \(A \sqsubseteq \exists r.\neg A\)). This causes an inescapable logical loop, breaking any potential model.

Decide whether \(\forall s.\bot\) is satisfiable w.r.t. the TBox \(\mathcal{T}_2\) from Exercise 7.

Solution Breakdown

Part 1 w.r.t. \(\mathcal{T}_1\)

(Recall: In \(\mathcal{T}_1\), \(B \equiv \bot\))

  1. \(B \sqcup \forall r.B \equiv \bot \sqcup \forall r.\bot \equiv \forall r.\bot\): Satisfiable (by a node with no \(r\)-successors).
  2. \(\neg B \sqcap \exists r.\bot \equiv \neg B \sqcap \bot \equiv \bot\): Unsatisfiable.
  3. \(\exists r.\exists r.B \equiv \exists r.\bot \equiv \bot\): Unsatisfiable.

Part 2 w.r.t. \(\mathcal{T}_2\)

Decide if \(\forall s.\bot\) is satisfiable.

  • Result: Unsatisfiable.
  • Proof: Assume node \(x \in \forall s.\bot\). This means \(x\) has NO \(s\)-successors. Because it has no \(s\)-successors, \(\forall s.\neg B\) is vacuously True for \(x\). By the rule \(\forall s.\neg B \sqsubseteq A\), node \(x\) must be in \(A\). However, the rule \(A \sqsubseteq \exists s.B\) states anything in \(A\) must have an \(s\)-successor. A node cannot simultaneously have zero \(s\)-successors and at least one \(s\)-successor.

Exercise 9. Subsumption

Check whether the following subsumption relations hold w.r.t. the TBox \(\mathcal{T}_1\) from Exercise 7.

  1. \(\exists r.(A \sqcap B) \sqsubseteq \forall r.A\)
  2. \(A \sqcup B \sqsubseteq \forall r.B\)
  3. \(\exists s.B \sqsubseteq \exists r.\neg B\)

Repeat the exercise now using the TBox \(\mathcal{T}_2\) from Exercise 7.

Solution Breakdown

Part 1 w.r.t. \(\mathcal{T}_1\)

(Recall: \(B \equiv \bot\))

  1. \(\exists r.(A \sqcap B) \sqsubseteq \forall r.A\): YES. LHS contains \(B\), so LHS is \(\bot\). \(\bot\) is subsumed by anything.
  2. \(A \sqcup B \sqsubseteq \forall r.B\): NO. \(A \sqcup B\) simplifies to \(A\). \(A\) is not forced to only have \(r\)-successors in \(B\) (which is \(\bot\)).
  3. \(\exists s.B \sqsubseteq \exists r.\neg B\): YES. LHS is \(\bot\).

Part 2 w.r.t. \(\mathcal{T}_2\)

(In \(\mathcal{T}_2\), \(\exists r.B \sqsubseteq \forall r.\neg B\) forces \(\exists r.B\) to be \(\bot\). A node cannot have an \(r\)-successor in \(B\) while simultaneously demanding all its \(r\)-successors be \(\neg B\)).

  1. \(\exists r.(A \sqcap B) \sqsubseteq \forall r.A\): YES. LHS is \(\bot\).
  2. \(A \sqcup B \sqsubseteq \forall r.B\): NO. Instances of \(A\) can have \(r\)-successors as long as they are not \(B\).
  3. \(\exists s.B \sqsubseteq \exists r.\neg B\): NO. A node can have an \(s\)-successor in \(B\) but have zero \(r\)-successors.
